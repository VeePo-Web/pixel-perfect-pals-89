# Manitoba Region Fact Bundles (computed once — §4C)

Shared provincial facts (usable on any page, sparingly):
- Manitoba 2021 census population: 1,342,153 (StatCan). Winnipeg CSD 749,607 — over half the province.
- Climate province-wide: extreme continental. Winnipeg averages ~ -16°C January mean, +20°C July mean; 50°C+ annual temperature swing; deep frost penetration (~2.4 m design frost depth in southern MB); freeze-thaw shoulder seasons; heavy snow loads; short intense summer construction season.
- Southern Manitoba = former glacial Lake Agassiz lakebed: heavy expansive clay ("Red River gumbo") that swells wet / shrinks dry — foundation movement, basement cracking, sump/backup issues are region-defining property conditions.
- Red River spring flooding is a recurring reality (1950, 1997 "Flood of the Century", 2009, 2011); Red River Floodway ("Duff's Ditch", completed 1968, expanded 2000s) protects Winnipeg; valley towns rely on ring dikes.
- Provincial hubs for distance fields: Winnipeg (49.8951, -97.1384) and Brandon (49.8483, -99.9501).

## Winnipeg Metro (CD 11)
- Scope here: Winnipeg CSD + Headingley RM (+ Winnipeg's neighborhood tier).
- Economy: provincial capital; aerospace (StandardAero, Magellan, Boeing Winnipeg), transportation/logistics (CentrePort Canada inland port, CN Symington & CP yards, Winnipeg Richardson International Airport), finance/insurance (Great-West Life/Canada Life, IG Wealth, Wawanesa), agri-food (Richardson International HQ), healthcare (Health Sciences Centre, St. Boniface Hospital), CFB Winnipeg/17 Wing.
- Landmarks: The Forks (junction of Red & Assiniboine rivers), Canadian Museum for Human Rights, Exchange District National Historic Site (early-1900s warehouse architecture), Assiniboine Park & Zoo, Portage & Main.
- Geography: pancake-flat Red River Valley clay plain at the Red–Assiniboine confluence; elm-canopied mature streets; two river systems with riverbank properties.
- Building stock: pre-1920 brick-and-timber (Exchange, Wolseley, St. Boniface), interwar/postwar bungalows (Kildonans, St. James, East Kildonan), 1960s–80s suburbs (St. Vital, Fort Richmond, Charleswood), 2000s+ master-planned growth (Waverley West/Bridgwater, Sage Creek); LUD/estate lots in Headingley.
- Property conditions: Red River gumbo clay + mature-tree root systems + freeze-thaw = the classic Winnipeg foundation/teleposts story; older stock on wood or concrete teleposts; floodway protection since 1968.

## Eastman (CDs 1, 2, 12)
- Scope: southeastern Manitoba — Steinbach, Hanover, Taché (Lorette, Landmark), Ritchot (St. Adolphe, Île-des-Chênes), Niverville, La Broquerie, Ste. Anne, De Salaberry (St. Malo), St-Pierre-Jolys, Springfield (Oakbank, Dugald), Brokenhead/Beausejour, Lac du Bonnet, Pinawa, Powerview-Pine Falls, Whitemouth, Stuartburn, Piney, Reynolds.
- Economy: Manitoba's fastest-growing belt — Steinbach (3rd-largest city; automotive dealerships, trucking, manufacturing, Mennonite-rooted entrepreneurship), Niverville (one of Canada's fastest-growing communities, +28% 2016→2021 per census), commuter growth into SE Winnipeg; Springfield hosts rural-industrial parks on the Winnipeg boundary; Pinawa = Whiteshell Laboratories (AECL) science town; Powerview-Pine Falls = former paper-mill town on the Winnipeg River hydro corridor.
- Geography: transitions from Red River Valley clay (west: Niverville, Ritchot) through sandy loam and bush (La Broquerie, Ste. Anne) to Canadian Shield granite and boreal forest (Lac du Bonnet, Pinawa, Whiteshell); Winnipeg River hydro dams (Pine Falls, Great Falls, Seven Sisters).
- Landmarks: Mennonite Heritage Village (Steinbach), Whiteshell Provincial Park edge, Winnipeg River, St. Malo Provincial Park & grotto, Crow Wing Trail (historic Red River ox-cart route through the southeast), Dawson Trail (Canada's first all-Canadian route west, through Ste. Anne/Lorette country).
- Building stock: new-build subdivisions (Niverville, Oakbank, Lorette, Steinbach) alongside century farmyards; francophone parishes (St. Adolphe, St-Pierre-Jolys, Ste. Anne) with heritage cores; cottage/lake properties toward the Shield.
- Property conditions: west = gumbo clay + Red River flood plain (St. Adolphe ring dike; Niverville near the dike network); east = Shield bedrock/thin soils, septic-and-well rural service.

## Interlake (CDs 13, 14, 18)
- Scope: between Lake Winnipeg and Lake Manitoba — Selkirk, St. Andrews, St. Clements, East/West St. Paul, Stonewall (with Stony Mountain), Rockwood, Rosser, Teulon, Woodlands (Warren), Gimli, Winnipeg Beach, Arborg, Fisher, Armstrong, St. Laurent, Coldwell (Lundar), Grahamdale, Dunnottar.
- Economy: Selkirk steel (Gerdau Manitoba mill) and regional healthcare; Stony Mountain Institution (federal penitentiary, 1877); CentrePort-adjacent industrial growth in Rosser; Gimli = Diageo distillery (Crown Royal) + fishing fleet; commercial fishing on Lake Winnipeg (Gimli, Riverton); Icelandic settlement heritage ("New Iceland" — Gimli, Arborg, Riverton); Métis community of St. Laurent on Lake Manitoba.
- Geography: flat lacustrine plain over Ordovician limestone/dolomite bedrock — Tyndall stone quarried at Garson/Tyndall (the mottled limestone in the Manitoba Legislature and Parliament); Netley-Libau Marsh at the Red River mouth; beaches along Lake Winnipeg's west shore (Winnipeg Beach, Matlock, Sandy Hook).
- Landmarks: Lower Fort Garry National Historic Site (St. Andrews — oldest intact stone fur-trade fort in North America), St. Andrews-on-the-Red (oldest stone church in Western Canada still in use), Red River Floodway outlet at Lockport (Lockport lock & dam), New Iceland Heritage Museum (Gimli), Oak Hammock Marsh.
- Building stock: riverfront estates along River Road (St. Andrews/St. Clements), cottage-to-year-round conversions on the lake shores, Selkirk's early-1900s core, acreages on shallow limestone.
- Property conditions: high water table + shallow bedrock (blasting/rock excavation not unusual north of Stonewall); lake-effect wind/ice on shoreline properties; Red River bank stability at Lockport/Selkirk.

## Central Plains (CDs 8, 9, 10)
- Scope: Portage la Prairie (city + RM), Grey, North Norfolk (MacGregor), Victoria, Macdonald (La Salle, Oak Bluff, Sanford, Starbuck), Cartier (Elie), St. François Xavier.
- Economy: Portage la Prairie = agri-food processing capital (McCain Foods and Simplot french-fry plants, Roquette's pea-protein plant — one of the world's largest), Southport Aerospace (former CFB); vegetable/potato farming on the Portage plains — some of Canada's richest soil; Macdonald RM = Winnipeg's southwest growth fringe (Oak Bluff, La Salle).
- Geography: the Portage plains — table-flat, stone-free lacustrine loam between the Assiniboine River and Lake Manitoba; Assiniboine River diversion (Portage Diversion) protects against Assiniboine floods; La Salle River winds through Macdonald RM.
- Landmarks: Fort la Reine Museum (Portage), Island Park & Crescent Lake (an oxbow "lake within a city"), St. François Xavier's White Horse legend statue on the Trans-Canada, historic Red River Métis river lots along the Assiniboine.
- Building stock: Portage's tree-lined early-1900s Crescent-area homes; new estates in Oak Bluff/La Salle; farmsteads on section roads.
- Property conditions: Red River gumbo east (La Salle/Oak Bluff — same clay as Winnipeg), Assiniboine loam west; spring overland flooding managed by the Portage Diversion; high winds across open plains.

## Pembina Valley (CDs 3, 4)
- Scope: Winkler, Morden, Stanley, Altona, Rhineland, Morris (town + RM), Carman, Dufferin, Montcalm, Roland, Thompson RM (Miami), Lorne, Louise, Pembina (Manitou), Argyle, Emerson-Franklin.
- Economy: Canada's densest Mennonite settlement belt and one of rural Canada's fastest-growing corridors — Winkler (manufacturing: trailers, agriculture equipment; Triple E RV legacy) and Morden (Pembina Valley's civic twin; Canadian Fossil Discovery Centre) both grew ~10%+ 2016→2021; Altona = Friesens Corporation (Canada's largest book printer) + sunflower country; Carman = seed/agriculture research (U of M research farm); Morris = Manitoba Stampede rodeo town at the Morris/Red junction; Emerson = Canada's busiest prairie land border crossing on I-29/Hwy 75.
- Geography: Red River Valley floor east (Altona, Morris, Emerson — ring-diked towns on gumbo clay) rising west up the Manitou Escarpment (Pembina Hills) — Morden/Winkler sit at the escarpment toe; the Pembina Valley itself is a glacial spillway gorge cut 100+ m into the escarpment (La Rivière ski hill country).
- Landmarks: Canadian Fossil Discovery Centre (Morden — largest mosasaur collection in Canada, "Bruce" the 13-m mosasaur), Morris Stampede grounds, Pembina Valley Provincial Park, Stephenfield Lake (Carman's reservoir), the Hwy 75 flood-proofed corridor.
- Building stock: booming new subdivisions (Winkler/Morden/Altona), housebarn-heritage villages on the West Reserve (Neubergthal National Historic Site near Altona — best-preserved Mennonite street village in North America), ring-diked town cores (Morris, Emerson, St. Jean Baptiste).
- Property conditions: valley floor = deepest gumbo clay + Red River flood plain (1997 flood zone; ring dikes at Morris/Emerson/St. Jean); escarpment towns = better drainage but slope/runoff; artesian groundwater quirks near the hills.

## Westman (CDs 5, 6, 7, 15)
- Scope: Brandon, Cornwallis (Shilo area), Whitehead, Elton, Carberry, Riverdale (Rivers), Virden, Sifton, Pipestone, Sioux Valley Dakota Nation, Melita, Killarney-Turtle Mountain, Neepawa, Minnedosa, Rosedale, Hamiota, Oakview.
- Economy: Brandon = Manitoba's second city ("Wheat City") — Maple Leaf Foods pork plant (one of Canada's largest), Assiniboine College & Brandon University, Royal Manitoba Winter Fair; CFB Shilo (artillery base) east of Brandon; Virden = Manitoba's oil patch (Williston Basin pumpjacks since the 1950s); Neepawa = HyLife pork processing + "Lily Capital of the World" + Margaret Laurence's hometown; Carberry = potato country (McCain processing links) beside Spruce Woods; Killarney/Melita = southwest farm-service towns; Minnedosa = valley town with its namesake lake.
- Geography: rolling till plains and the broad Assiniboine River valley (Brandon's valley setting); Brandon Hills; Carberry Sandhills/Spruce Woods (Spirit Sands open dunes); Turtle Mountain plateau on the US line; Riding Mountain escarpment rising north of Neepawa/Minnedosa.
- Landmarks: Spirit Sands & Devil's Punch Bowl (Spruce Woods), Riding Mountain National Park south gate country, International Peace Garden (Turtle Mountain), Daly House Museum & Brandon's historic Rosser Avenue, Criddle/Vane Homestead.
- Building stock: Brandon's Victorian/Edwardian core + postwar wards + new south-end growth; PMQ-style housing at Shilo; solid interwar brick mains streets (Virden's sandstone-trimmed downtown, Neepawa's Mountain Avenue).
- Property conditions: sandier, better-drained soils than the Red River Valley (less gumbo, more frost-heave on till and sand); Assiniboine valley slope stability & 2011 flood memory (Brandon's 18th Street dikes); wind-driven snow loading on open prairie.

## Parkland (CDs 16, 17, 20)
- Scope: Dauphin (city + RM), Ste. Rose du Lac, Alonsa, Gilbert Plains, Grandview, Mossey River (Fork River/Winnipegosis edge), McCreary, Rossburn, Swan River, and the Duck Mountain fringe.
- Economy: Dauphin = Parkland's service capital (healthcare, government, agriculture) and home of Canada's National Ukrainian Festival + Dauphin's Countryfest (Canada's largest country music festival); Swan Valley = grain/canola + forestry (Louisiana-Pacific at Minitonas historically); Ste. Rose = "Cattle Capital of Manitoba" auction mart; strong Ukrainian settlement heritage across the region (onion-domed churches).
- Geography: aspen-parkland transition between prairie and boreal; framed by Riding Mountain (south) and Duck Mountain Provincial Park (north — Baldy Mountain, Manitoba's highest point, 831 m); lakes Dauphin, Winnipegosis, Manitoba (west shores); Vermillion River through Dauphin.
- Landmarks: Riding Mountain National Park (Wasagaming/Clear Lake) gateway towns, Duck Mountain/Baldy Mountain, Fort Dauphin Museum, Selo Ukraina festival site, Asessippi ski valley to the southwest.
- Building stock: Dauphin's early-1900s railway-town core (CN roundhouse heritage), farmsteads and lakefront cottages (Winnipegosis, Ste. Rose's Lake Manitoba shore), hunting/fishing camps toward the Ducks.
- Property conditions: parkland till + pockets of lacustrine clay near the lakes; heavier snowpack near the mountains; remote-lot access and seasonal cottage systems (wells, septic, wood heat).

## Northern Manitoba (CDs 19, 21, 22, 23)
- Scope: Thompson, The Pas, Flin Flon (Manitoba part), Norway House, Peguis (CD19 Fisher River country), plus deferred fly-in/rail communities (Churchill, Gillam, Snow Lake, Lynn Lake, Cross Lake, Moose Lake…).
- Economy: mining and hydro — Thompson (Vale nickel operations; founded 1956 as a purpose-built mining city), Flin Flon (copper-zinc; Hudbay heritage; city named after a pulp-novel character, Flintabbatey Flonatin), The Pas (forestry, transportation gateway; Opaskwayak Cree Nation adjacent with its Otineka Mall — one of the first First Nation-owned malls in Canada); Manitoba Hydro dams on the Nelson/Saskatchewan rivers; Norway House = one of Manitoba's largest First Nations (historic HBC inland headquarters at the top of Lake Winnipeg); University College of the North campuses (The Pas, Thompson).
- Geography: Canadian Shield granite, boreal forest, muskeg and discontinuous permafrost; thousands of lakes; the Nelson River draining Lake Winnipeg to Hudson Bay; winter-road network to remote communities.
- Landmarks: Pisew Falls (Manitoba's most famous waterfall, near Thompson), Clearwater Lake Provincial Park (The Pas — "the lake you can see through"), Flin Flon's rock-perched houses and Flinty statue, Norway House's historic Archway Warehouse, Thompson's Spirit Way wolf mural.
- Building stock: 1950s–70s company-town housing (Thompson, Flin Flon — homes stepped into Shield rock, some with above-ground utilidors), band housing and log heritage at Norway House, rail-town stock in The Pas.
- Property conditions: bedrock at/near surface (no basements in much of Flin Flon; blasting for services), muskeg settlement, permafrost pockets, -40° winters with extreme freeze-up, materials logistics by rail/winter road — response times and mobilization are a real differentiator.
