## About Pembina Valley

The Pembina Valley is south-central Manitoba's Red River Valley west side — Canada's densest Mennonite settlement belt and one of rural Canada's fastest-growing corridors. Twin cities Winkler (13,745) and Morden (9,929) anchor it at the toe of the Manitou Escarpment, with Altona's printing-and-sunflower economy, ring-diked Morris and Emerson on the Highway 75 flood plain, and Carman's seed-research farms to the north. The region rises from deep gumbo clay on the valley floor to the glacial-spillway gorge of the Pembina River cutting through the Pembina Hills.

## Across the Pembina Valley region

The Pembina Valley is Manitoba's proof that 'rural' and 'booming' can be the same place. Winkler (13,745, one of Manitoba's fastest-growing cities) and Morden (9,929) sit ten kilometres apart where the Red River Valley floor meets the Manitou Escarpment, surrounded by the RM of Stanley's expanding fringe. Manufacturing — trailers, agricultural equipment, the legacy of Triple E's RV plants — gives Winkler an industrial base few towns its size can match, while Morden pairs civic growth with the Canadian Fossil Discovery Centre, home to 'Bruce,' the 13-metre mosasaur that headlines Canada's largest marine-reptile collection. East of the twin cities, the valley floor flattens into some of the deepest expansive clay in Manitoba. Altona — sunflower capital and home of Friesens Corporation, Canada's largest book printer — anchors the West Reserve, where street villages like Neubergthal (a National Historic Site, the best-preserved Mennonite housebarn village in North America) still line the section roads. Farther east on Highway 75, Morris and Emerson live inside ring dikes that seal shut when the Red River rises — Morris famously hosts the Manitoba Stampede rodeo on ground that floods in big years. {COMPANY_NAME} plans dike-town projects around flood-zone drainage rules and staging access as a matter of course.

West of the twins, the land climbs the escarpment into the Pembina Hills, where the Pembina River's glacial-spillway gorge cuts more than a hundred metres deep — ski-hill country at La Rivière, orchard-and-pasture slopes around Manitou and the Lorne, Louise and Pembina municipalities. Slope drainage, artesian groundwater quirks and wind exposure shape the work up here, a different world from the gumbo below.

Carman and Dufferin round out the region's north with seed-research farms and one of rural Manitoba's stronger service towns.
