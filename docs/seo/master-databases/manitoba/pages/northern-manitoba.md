## About Northern Manitoba

Northern Manitoba is the province's Canadian Shield half — boreal forest, muskeg and thousands of lakes drained by the Nelson River toward Hudson Bay. Its economy runs on mining and hydro: Thompson (13,035) was purpose-built in 1956 for Vale's nickel operations; Flin Flon mines copper-zinc from rock so bare its houses step up granite outcrops; The Pas anchors forestry and rail at the region's southern gateway beside Opaskwayak Cree Nation; and Norway House, at the top of Lake Winnipeg, grew from the Hudson's Bay Company's historic inland headquarters into one of Manitoba's largest First Nations communities.

## Across the Northern Manitoba region

Northern Manitoba covers more ground than the rest of the province combined, and every {SERVICE} decision up here traces back to the Canadian Shield in this part of Manitoba. Thompson (13,035) — the 'Hub of the North,' purpose-built in 1956 when Inco (now Vale) opened the nickel belt — carries the region's largest housing stock: planned-city blocks from the 1950s–70s now into their renewal decades, plus the commercial base that serves communities for hundreds of kilometres. Spirit Way's giant wolf mural and the highway to Pisew Falls give the city its icons; its property market gives crews steady work re-detailing mid-century construction for modern loads.

Flin Flon, the copper-zinc city on the Saskatchewan border (this service area covers the Manitoba part — the boundary runs through town), is the Shield made visible: streets climb bare granite, homes anchor to outcrop, and much of the older stock was built without basements because there is simply no digging here. Sewer and water in parts of town historically ran in above-ground utilidor boxes — a reminder that services behave differently on rock. The Pas, the southern gateway where the rail lines meet, blends forestry, transportation and University College of the North, with Opaskwayak Cree Nation across the river operating the landmark Otineka Mall. Norway House Cree Nation (about 5,750 in the community), at Lake Winnipeg's northern outlet, grew from the Hudson's Bay Company's inland headquarters — its historic Archway Warehouse still stands — into one of Manitoba's largest First Nations, and Peguis First Nation anchors the region's southeast reach.
