## About Parkland

The Parkland is west-central Manitoba's aspen-parkland belt, framed by Riding Mountain National Park to the south and Duck Mountain Provincial Park — home of Baldy Mountain, Manitoba's highest point at 831 m — to the north. Dauphin (8,368), the regional capital, hosts Canada's National Ukrainian Festival and Dauphin's Countryfest, the country's largest country-music festival; Swan River anchors the grain-and-forestry valley farther north, and Ste. Rose du Lac runs the province's best-known cattle auction. Onion-domed Ukrainian churches mark the settlement heritage across the region.

## Across the Parkland region

The Parkland is the Manitoba that visitors from the flat south don't expect: aspen bluffs rolling between two upland parks, with Baldy Mountain in Duck Mountain Provincial Park standing 831 metres — the province's roof. Dauphin (8,368) is the region's service capital, its early-1900s railway-town core (the CN heritage runs deep here) hosting the hospitals, government offices and retail for a huge catchment, and its festival calendar hosting the country twice over: Canada's National Ukrainian Festival on Selo Ukraina's hillside site and Dauphin's Countryfest, the largest country-music festival in Canada. The Vermillion River threads the city, and Ukrainian settlement heritage — onion-domed churches on section roads — defines the cultural landscape from Rossburn to Gilbert Plains and Grandview along Highway 5.

For {SERVICE}, the Parkland splits into three kinds of work in this corner of Manitoba. Town cores — Dauphin, Swan River (4,049), Ste. Rose du Lac, Grandview — carry solid interwar housing and main-street commercial stock that reward era-aware methods. Farm country runs on parkland till with pockets of lacustrine clay near lakes Dauphin, Manitoba and Winnipegosis, where {COMPANY_NAME} scopes farmstead projects around outbuildings, long approaches and stock-water systems; Ste. Rose's 'Cattle Capital of Manitoba' auction mart says everything about the local economy. And the lake-and-mountain fringe — Winnipegosis waterfront, cabins toward the Ducks, and the Clear Lake gateway country south of McCreary — brings classic cottage scope: wells, septic fields, wood heat and access roads that mud out in spring.

Climate sets the Parkland apart from southern Manitoba in one big way: snow. The mountain fringe catches a heavier, longer-lasting snowpack than the open prairie, so roof loading, spring melt drainage and shoulder-season access get engineered into every estimate rather than discovered in April.
