## About Manitoba

Manitoba is Canada's keystone province: 1,342,153 people (2021 census) centred on Winnipeg, where more than half the population lives, with Brandon anchoring the southwest and Thompson the north. Its geography runs from the table-flat Red River Valley — former glacial Lake Agassiz lakebed with its famous expansive clay — through limestone Interlake lowlands and aspen Parkland to Canadian Shield granite covering the province's northern half. The economy blends agriculture and agri-food processing, aerospace, transportation-logistics, mining and hydro power.

## Serving the whole province

Manitoba looks simple on a map and is anything but simple under a property. {COMPANY_NAME} serves the whole province — 1,342,153 people across communities from the U.S. line to the northern Shield — and organizes {SERVICE} into eight regions because Manitoba's ground genuinely changes that much.

The Winnipeg Metro Region holds more than half the province on the bed of glacial Lake Agassiz, where expansive Red River gumbo moves foundations seasonally and housing spans 140 years of construction eras. Eastman, the fast-growing southeast, runs from that same clay through Steinbach's boom belt to Canadian Shield cottage country. The Interlake stretches between Lake Winnipeg and Lake Manitoba on shallow limestone — Tyndall-stone country — with steel-town Selkirk and the Icelandic lakeshore at Gimli. Central Plains pairs Portage la Prairie's food-processing plants with Winnipeg's southwest estate fringe.

South along the Red, the Pembina Valley is rural Canada's growth story — Winkler, Morden and Altona — with ring-diked river towns on the Highway 75 flood plain. Westman, centred on Brandon, swaps clay for sandy till and adds the Assiniboine valley's slopes; the Parkland works farm, festival and cottage country between Riding Mountain and Duck Mountain; and Northern Manitoba runs on mining-town housing, First Nations communities, bedrock, muskeg and -40 winters from The Pas to Thompson.
