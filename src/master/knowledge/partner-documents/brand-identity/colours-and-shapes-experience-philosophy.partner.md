# Partner: Colours & Shapes — Experience Philosophy (Goodness / Truth / Beauty)

## 1. Document title
**Colours & Shapes — Anthony Diehl Experience Design Philosophy** (originally framed for Christian / church organizations)

## 2. Source reference
[`colours-and-shapes-experience-philosophy.source.md`](../../source-documents/brand-identity/colours-and-shapes-experience-philosophy.source.md)

## 3. Document category & scope
- **Category:** Brand Identity → Experience Philosophy
- **Scope:** GLOBAL philosophy with **mandatory adaptation**. The source document is framed for Christian organizations (churches, ministries, liturgical color logic, scripture pull-quotes). For Cochrane Master Builders and other secular brands, the *underlying principles* apply — the *theological vocabulary does not*.

## 4. Main purpose
To install the "Goodness, Truth, and Beauty" experience philosophy: design as hospitality, mission-first decision making, manifesto-driven brand systems, and weaving recurring visual motifs that embody core values. It models how to translate deep belief into design.

## 5. What this document influences
- The "start with why" discipline before any aesthetic choice
- Manifesto-driven design systems (define behaviours → reflect them in micro-interactions and imagery)
- Recurring motif strategy (e.g., Centre Church's "fibre" — translate to Cochrane's bespoke motif)
- Story-driven experiences: testimonials, transformation, narrative homepage flow
- Empathy-first audience segmentation
- Color emotional/symbolic logic (for secular brands: translate from liturgical → brand-emotional palette)
- Performance, accessibility, mobile-first as expressions of care

## 6. Trigger prompts / keywords
Consult for: "experience philosophy", "design hospitality", "mission-first", "manifesto", "brand motif", "story-driven", "purpose before pixels", "why this design choice matters", "visual signature element", "color symbolism", "emotional resonance", or whenever a brand asks for design that "means something" beyond aesthetics.

## 7. How it should guide output quality
- Refuse to start with aesthetics; demand a stated mission/why first.
- Treat color as carrying meaning, not decoration.
- Build a manifesto-derived behaviour list and check designs against it.
- Identify or invent a recurring motif that threads through logo → site → micro-interactions.
- Use authentic photography (not stock) of the actual subject.
- Tell stories of transformation prominently.

## 8. Relationship to brand identity / ICP / conversion
Pairs with `brand-identity-architect.partner.md` as the **soul layer** beneath the strategic identity work. While the Architect builds the system, this document insists the system have meaning. For Cochrane Master Builders, that means the bespoke craft, family legacy, and trust narrative carry through every motif and micro-interaction.

## 9. Conflict-resolution rules
- **Brand-specific documents always win** on actual palette, typography, motif choice, and tone.
- **This document is reference-only** for principles — never import its Christian-specific vocabulary, scripture references, liturgical color guides, or church-context examples into Cochrane Master Builders output.
- When this document recommends "scripture quote in hero", translate to the equivalent: a brand truth statement, founder principle, or craft motto.

## 10. Dependencies & related partner docs
- `brand-identity-architect.partner.md` — strategic backbone.
- `master-design-persona-fantasy.partner.md` — execution craft.
- All Cochrane Master Builders brand documents — final authority on actual identity.

## 11. Practical "when to use" examples
- Designing a new section that needs emotional weight → consult the manifesto + motif principles.
- Choosing imagery direction → apply the "authentic photography of actual subject" rule.
- Reviewing a homepage → check that it tells a transformation story, not just lists features.
- Picking a color → ask "what does this mean for *this* brand", not just "is it pretty".

## 12. Anti-patterns this document protects against
Aesthetic-first decisions with no purpose, stock photography that disconnects from the actual subject, decorative color with no meaning, feature-list homepages with no story, generic micro-interactions, ignoring accessibility as a values question, and importing this document's religious vocabulary into secular brand work.
