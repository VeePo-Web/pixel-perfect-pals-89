# 1. Strategic Purpose

This document is the master design-persona style guide template for the Cochrane Master Builders website ecosystem. It gives the homepage and service pages a consistent visual system, then explains how to transform that system into bespoke, service-specific website experiences across the 115-service network.

The system is designed for a family-owned construction brand that must feel traditional in values, modern in usability, local in trust, structured in proof, and premium through clarity rather than flash.

# 2. Non-Negotiable Design Principles

## Human Experience First

Every design choice should reduce homeowner anxiety: price uncertainty, contractor trust, project disruption, hidden conditions, permit confusion, and fear of bad work.

## Brand Truth Before Decoration

The brand must not look like a generic contractor template. It should feel like a serious local family builder with modern digital clarity.

## Clarity Is The Premium Cue

The website should feel expensive because it is useful, structured, calm, and precise. Do not rely on fake luxury effects.

## Service Specificity Wins Trust

Every service domain must explain the real issues that matter to that trade. A roofing page cannot feel like a bathroom page with swapped keywords.

## Performance Is Part Of The Brand

A slow, jumpy, overanimated page weakens trust. The site should feel fast, stable, and controlled on mobile.

# 4. Homepage Style Guide Template

The homepage is the master front door. It must make the broad service ecosystem feel controlled, trustworthy, and easy to enter. It should answer: who are you, what do you do, why should I trust you, what might this cost, and what happens next?

## 4.1 Top Trust Bar

Purpose: Quiet local assurance before the hero.

Style: Thin warm-neutral strip. Small text. No promo look.

Copy direction: Family-owned construction services for Cochrane homes, acreages, and surrounding communities.

Rule: Do not overload. Keep under one line on desktop where possible.

## 4.2 Navigation

Purpose: Make the service ecosystem feel organized.

Style: Sticky nav, warm white or navy, thin divider, clear labels, restrained CTA.

Copy direction: Services, Pricing, Process, Areas, Projects, About, Contact, Request A Clear Estimate.

Rule: Use a service mega-menu grouped by clusters, not a flat list of 115 links.

## 4.3 Hero

Purpose: Create instant clarity and trust.

Style: Split layout or editorial hero. Strong headline, service finder prompt, proof cards, clean imagery.

Copy direction: Cochrane’s Family-Owned Builders For Homes That Need To Last.

Rule: CTA must be visible above fold on mobile. No autoplay video.

## 4.4 Trust Signal Strip

Purpose: Reduce doubt immediately after hero.

Style: Four or five compact cards with icons or line marks.

Copy direction: Family-Owned. Clear Pricing Guidance. Local Cochrane Service. Long-Term Workmanship.

Rule: Keep text short. Trust is earned through specificity.

## 4.5 Service Finder

Purpose: Help users choose the correct service pathway.

Style: Search bar, cluster cards, popular service row, helper card.

Copy direction: Find The Right Service Under One Family-Owned Standard.

Rule: Make breadth feel useful, not overwhelming.

## 4.6 Pricing Guidance Preview

Purpose: Turn price anxiety into trust.

Style: Three calm pricing cards with cost-driver chips.

Copy direction: Clear Pricing Guidance Before You Start The Conversation.

Rule: Always use ranges and drivers, not careless guarantees.

## 4.7 Process Preview

Purpose: Show that the company is organized.

Style: Timeline cards. Desktop horizontal, mobile vertical.

Copy direction: A Clear Process From First Message To Final Walkthrough.

Rule: The process is a trust asset, not filler.

## 4.8 Family Legacy Block

Purpose: Make the brand emotionally different.

Style: Warm editorial block, restrained image, strong quote-style line.

Copy direction: Built Like It Still Matters 50 Years From Now.

Rule: Avoid cheesy stock family imagery. Use craft and local realism.

## 4.9 Proof / Project Framework

Purpose: Show evidence or prepare for proof collection.

Style: Case-story cards with challenge, approach, result.

Copy direction: Proof You Can See. Work You Can Live With.

Rule: If proof is missing, use an honest proof placeholder.

## 4.10 Areas We Serve

Purpose: Support local SEO and local reassurance.

Style: Community clusters, map-inspired visual, local copy, search chips.

Copy direction: Serving Cochrane And The Communities Around It.

Rule: Do not make this look like keyword stuffing.

## 4.11 FAQ Preview

Purpose: Answer objections before contact.

Style: Accordion with six to eight practical questions.

Copy direction: Questions Homeowners Ask Before Starting A Project.

Rule: Use visible FAQ schema later in implementation.

## 4.12 Final CTA

Purpose: Turn clarity into action.

Style: Strong calm panel. No fake urgency.

Copy direction: Ready To Build It Properly?

Rule: Make the next step safe: no pressure, send what you know.

# 5. Service Page Style Guide Template

The service page is a focused conversion page. It must make one service feel clear, safe, and worth contacting about. The visitor is no longer asking “what do you do?” They are asking, “can I trust you with this exact project?”

## 5.1 Service Hero

Purpose: Confirm exact service, location, and trust.

Style: Family-Owned \[Service\] In Cochrane

Copy direction: \[Service\] Built For Long-Term Value.

Rule: Use service-specific imagery and a service-specific CTA.

## 5.2 Fast Answer Block

Purpose: Give humans and search systems a clear answer.

Style: Answer card directly under hero.

Copy direction: \[Service\] in Cochrane usually involves...

Rule: Keep 50–90 words. Clear, useful, no fluff.

## 5.3 Problem / Stakes

Purpose: Show the customer you understand the real risk.

Style: Two-column problem and risk card layout.

Copy direction: The real risk is not just \[surface issue\].

Rule: Name hidden risks without fearmongering.

## 5.4 Services Offered

Purpose: List exact sub-services and use cases.

Style: Specific service cards with price ranges and outcomes.

Copy direction: \[Service\] Options For The Way Your Home Actually Needs To Work.

Rule: Avoid vague cards like quality, value, or solutions.

## 5.5 Pricing Guidance

Purpose: Pre-handle cost anxiety.

Style: Small, standard, premium/complex pricing cards.

Copy direction: What \[Service\] Can Cost In Cochrane.

Rule: Include drivers: size, access, materials, permits, hidden conditions.

## 5.6 Process

Purpose: Make starting feel safe.

Style: Service-specific process timeline.

Copy direction: How We Approach \[Service\] Projects.

Rule: Add trade-specific steps, not generic steps only.

## 5.7 Materials / Methods

Purpose: Educate and increase price tolerance.

Style: Comparison cards and proper-method notes.

Copy direction: Materials And Methods Matter More Than Most People Realize.

Rule: Explain hidden work in plain language.

## 5.8 Family Standard Applied

Purpose: Connect the service to the master promise.

Style: Editorial trust block.

Copy direction: Built Like Your Family Will Live With The Result For Years.

Rule: Make it practical, not sentimental.

## 5.9 Proof / Examples

Purpose: Support claims with real work or honest placeholder.

Style: Case-story cards, process photos, before/after.

Copy direction: Recent \[Service\] Work And Practical Results.

Rule: Never fake projects, reviews, or awards.

## 5.10 Areas Served

Purpose: Convert local service intent.

Style: Compact area chips and community clusters.

Copy direction: \[Service\] Across Cochrane And Surrounding Communities.

Rule: Use local context, not a raw keyword list.

## 5.11 FAQ

Purpose: Answer practical buying questions.

Style: Accordion with specific answers.

Copy direction: Questions Homeowners Ask About \[Service\].

Rule: Cover cost, timeline, prep, permits, repair vs replace, starting point.

## 5.12 Final CTA

Purpose: Convert after clarity.

Style: Service-specific CTA panel.

Copy direction: Ready To Talk Through Your \[Service\] Project?

Rule: Ask for project details, location, timeline, and photos if available.

# 6. How-To: Transform The Master Style Guide Into A Bespoke Service Domain

This is the repeatable workflow for turning the master system into a specific domain such as CochraneRoofingMasters.ca, CochraneBathroomRenoMasters.ca, CochraneConcreteMasters.ca, CochraneDeckMasters.ca, or any other service brand.

## Step 1: Identify The Service Emotion

Decide the emotional job of the service. Roofing is protection. Bathrooms are comfort and dignity. Concrete is strength. Landscaping is outdoor life. Commercial is continuity. This emotion controls visual tone, hero imagery, proof type, and CTA wording.

## Step 2: Identify The Customer’s Hidden Fear

Write the fear beneath the search. A roofing buyer fears leaks and damage. A basement suite buyer fears permit problems and wasted money. A bathroom buyer fears water damage and disruption. The page must answer that fear directly.

## Step 3: Identify The Hidden Work

List what determines whether the project lasts but is often invisible to the customer: flashing, waterproofing, subfloor prep, drainage, curing, egress, sound separation, ventilation, substrate, code, access, sequencing.

## Step 4: Choose The Service Palette Variation

Start with the master navy and warm neutral system. Then add a service-specific material accent: slate for roofing, stone/brass for bathrooms, aggregate for concrete, timber/green for decks and landscaping, steel/blue-grey for commercial and systems work.

## Step 5: Choose The Main Visual Object

Each service needs one primary visual anchor: roofline, tile detail, finished suite, concrete formwork, deck framing, window flashing, cabinet joinery, duct diagram, landscape plan, commercial floorplan.

## Step 6: Build The Hero Around The Buying Trigger

Use the service emotion and hidden fear to write the hero. The H1 should not be generic. It should make the customer feel this page understands their exact project.

## Step 7: Create The Service-Specific Component Stack

Add one or two bespoke components for the service. Roofing may need repair-vs-replace. Concrete may need base-prep explainer. Basement suites may need legal-suite readiness. Bathrooms may need waterproofing explainer.

## Step 8: Rewrite Pricing Around Real Drivers

Do not copy the same pricing paragraph across domains. Show the specific drivers for that service: pitch, substrate, access, demolition, materials, permits, trades, season, hidden conditions.

## Step 9: Adapt Proof Requirements

Define what proof would matter for that service. Roofing needs inspection, flashing, roofline, cleanup. Bathrooms need waterproofing, tile alignment, fixtures. Concrete needs prep, forms, pour, cure, finish.

## Step 10: Create A Service-Specific CTA

The CTA should match customer readiness. Use Request A Roof Assessment, Plan Your Renovation, Request A Site Scope Review, Send Photos For A First Look, Talk Through A Commercial Scope, or Request A Clear Estimate.

# 7. How-To: Build A Bespoke Homepage For A Specific Service Domain

1.  Keep the global homepage order: trust bar, nav, hero, trust signals, service finder, pricing, process, legacy, proof, areas, FAQ, final CTA, footer.

2.  Change the hero from broad master-brand language to the exact service promise. Example: “Roofing Built To Protect The Family Under It.”

3.  Replace generic imagery with service-specific material evidence. Use real or staged details from the craft, not unrelated stock photos.

4.  Rewrite trust cards for the service. Roofing uses weather readiness and leak prevention. Bathrooms use waterproofing and daily comfort. Concrete uses base prep and drainage.

5.  Make the service finder smaller on specialist domains. Instead of every service, show main service, sub-services, related services, and “part of Cochrane Master Builders.”

6.  Use the pricing preview to show three service-specific ranges and the most important price drivers.

7.  Add one bespoke interactive or educational module above the fold or mid-page when useful.

8.  Keep the family legacy block consistent, but adapt the example to the service.

9.  Keep Areas We Serve consistent, but rewrite the local copy around service-specific conditions.

10. Make the final CTA service-specific and low-pressure.

# 8. How-To: Build A Bespoke Service Page

11. Start with the service name and domain. Confirm the exact primary keyword and service intent.

12. Write the service emotion in one word: protection, comfort, strength, relief, continuity, precision, flexibility, pride.

13. Write the hidden fear in one sentence. This becomes the problem/stakes section.

14. Write the hidden work list. This becomes the materials/methods section.

15. Write the offer menu with exact sub-services, use cases, and pricing ranges.

16. Write the pricing section around small, standard, and premium/complex scope.

17. Write the process section with universal steps plus service-specific steps.

18. Add the family-owned standard applied to this service: why this work matters to family life or long-term property value.

19. Add proof requirements. If proof is unavailable, use an honest future-proof framework.

20. Add FAQs that answer the questions someone would ask before hiring for this exact service.

21. Finish with one CTA. Do not overcomplicate the choice.

# 10. Shared Component Style Guide

## Hero

One clear H1, one trust subheadline, one primary CTA, one secondary CTA, optional proof cards.

## Cards

Slight radius, thin border, warm white fill, low shadow, no SaaS-like floating overload.

## Buttons

Primary CTA in navy or muted green. Secondary as border/text. No neon, no excessive glow.

## Forms

Multi-step on mobile. Explain why fields matter. Allow photo upload. Show calm success state.

## Pricing Cards

Use ranges, drivers, and assumptions. Do not present variable construction pricing as fixed promises.

## Accordions

Use for FAQs, price drivers, hidden work, and what to expect. Ensure keyboard accessibility.

## Proof Cards

Use real scope, location, challenge, approach, result. Do not publish fake project proof.

## Area Chips

Use local community names in a helpful way. Group by region. Avoid doorway-page feel.

# 11. Lovable / React Implementation Guidance

Build the website system as reusable templates powered by service configuration, not as 115 separate hand-coded sites. The style guide should become component logic.

- Use one shared homepage template with service-domain overrides.

- Use one shared service page template with service-specific config.

- Use content configs for hero copy, service offers, pricing, process, FAQs, related services, and areas served.

- Use design tokens for colour, spacing, radius, shadow, typography, and motion.

- Render critical content first: hero, answer block, service cards, pricing CTA.

- Lazy-load below-fold media and proof galleries.

- Use accessible accordions, forms, and navigation.

- Keep mobile CTAs sticky but calm: Call and Estimate.

- Track domain/source/service in hidden form fields.

- Use schema-ready content structures for LocalBusiness, Service, FAQPage, BreadcrumbList, and Organization where appropriate.

# 12. Homepage QA Checklist

- Does the first screen explain the master brand and why it matters?

- Is the family-owned standard visible or clearly implied?

- Can a user find a service path in under 10 seconds?

- Is pricing guidance visible before the contact form?

- Does the process section reduce fear?

- Does the page feel premium through clarity, not decoration?

- Are local areas visible without keyword stuffing?

- Are CTAs clear, low-pressure, and above the fold on mobile?

- Does the page avoid fake claims and unsupported badges?

- Does it load quickly and remain stable on mobile?

# 13. Service Page QA Checklist

- Is the exact service obvious in the hero?

- Does the page explain the real customer fear for this service?

- Does it explain hidden work that affects durability?

- Does it list specific services offered and price ranges?

- Does pricing include cost drivers and assumptions?

- Does the process section include service-specific details?

- Does the page connect the service to family-owned long-term value?

- Does it include proof or an honest proof-building framework?

- Does the FAQ answer real buyer questions?

- Does the CTA match the service and buying stage?

- Does the page avoid duplicated generic copy across domains?

- Does it feel useful enough to deserve ranking?

# 14. Final Design North Star

The homepage should make the visitor trust the company. The service page should make the visitor trust the company for one specific project. The how-to system should make every specialist domain feel bespoke while still belonging to the same family-owned builders’ network.
