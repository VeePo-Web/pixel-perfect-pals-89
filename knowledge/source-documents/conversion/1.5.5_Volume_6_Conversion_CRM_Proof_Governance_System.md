## 1. Conversion Spine

### 1.1 The Conversion Philosophy

Most contractor websites use conversion like a trap:

- "Get a free quote."
- "Book now."
- "Call today."
- "Limited spots available."

That may create form fills, but it does not automatically create trust, fit, or profitable work.

Cochrane Master Builders should use conversion as guidance.

A conversion event should answer:

- What does the customer need?
- Is this service a fit?
- What is the project stage?
- What information is still missing?
- What risk or permit issues may matter?
- What next step is responsible?
- How should the lead be routed?
- What proof can be collected later?

### 1.2 Conversion Promise

The user should feel:

"I know what to do next, and I understand why they are asking for this information."

This matters because construction buyers often feel vulnerable. They worry about being pressured, ignored, underquoted, misunderstood, or trapped in a vague process.

### 1.3 The Conversion Job

The website should convert users into the **right next action**, not always the same action.

Possible next actions:

- call now;
- request a scope review;
- upload photos;
- use the service finder;
- download a checklist;
- read pricing guidance;
- book a planning conversation;
- ask a permit-sensitive question;
- join future planning follow-up;
- receive a not-yet planning guide.

### 1.4 Conversion Must Protect Fit

A good conversion system attracts the right customer and politely filters the wrong one.

High-fit customer:

- values scope clarity;
- accepts pricing variables;
- respects permits where relevant;
- wants documentation;
- cares about long-term value;
- wants one accountable standard.

Low-fit customer:

- wants the cheapest number instantly;
- wants to skip permits or documentation;
- refuses scope review for complex work;
- pressures unrealistic deadlines;
- wants verbal-only promises;
- treats trades as disposable.

The conversion system should not be afraid to reduce bad-fit volume.

## 2. Evidence Boundary and No-Fake-Conversion Rule

### 2.1 What This Volume Does Not Claim

This volume does not claim that Cochrane Master Builders currently has:

- a specific CRM platform;
- specific conversion rates;
- specific close rates;
- specific response times;
- specific review count;
- specific project history;
- specific staffing levels;
- specific warranty terms;
- specific licenses or insurance;
- specific automated workflows already implemented.

It defines the system that should be built.

### 2.2 What Conversion Copy Must Not Fake

Do not use conversion language that implies:

- guaranteed quotes;
- instant pricing;
- same-day service;
- emergency response;
- fixed timelines;
- permit approval;
- full availability;
- warranty coverage;
- insurance/licensing proof;
- review volume;
- project history;
- "most trusted" status.

Unless verified.

### 2.3 Safe Conversion Language

Use:

- "request a scope review";
- "tell us what you are planning";
- "upload photos if available";
- "we will help identify the responsible next step";
- "pricing depends on scope and conditions";
- "some projects may require permit or trade review";
- "we will confirm what is known and what still needs review."

### 2.4 Conversion Integrity Test

Before launching any CTA, ask:

1. Is this action truthful?
2. Does the next step match what operations can actually do?
3. Does it reduce uncertainty or create pressure?
4. Does it help qualify the lead?
5. Does it set expectations clearly?
6. Does it protect the family standard?
7. Would a customer feel respected after clicking it?

## 3. The Customer Decision Journey

### 3.1 The Real Journey

The customer does not move neatly from homepage to contact form.

They often move through:

1. private concern;
2. Google search;
3. competitor comparison;
4. review reading;
5. pricing anxiety;
6. spouse/partner discussion;
7. photo taking;
8. local Facebook or neighbour asking;
9. website return;
10. form fill or call;
11. waiting for response;
12. quote comparison;
13. trust decision;
14. scope negotiation;
15. project decision.

The conversion system must support that reality.

### 3.2 Decision States

**State 1: Exploring**

Customer thought:

"I may need this later. I am trying to understand what is involved."

Best CTA:

- Learn What Affects Cost
- Download The Planning Checklist
- Explore The Service Path

CRM status:

- Future planning
- Low urgency
- Nurture

**State 2: Problem-Aware**

Customer thought:

"I know I need something done, but I do not know who to trust."

Best CTA:

- Find Your Project
- Request A Scope Review
- Ask About This Service

CRM status:

- New inquiry
- Needs qualification

**State 3: Price-Aware**

Customer thought:

"I need to know whether this is realistic."

Best CTA:

- See What Affects Cost
- Request Pricing Guidance
- Upload Project Photos

CRM status:

- Pricing guidance needed
- Scope unknown

**State 4: Risk-Aware**

Customer thought:

"What if permits, hidden conditions, or bad workmanship create problems later?"

Best CTA:

- Review Permit-Aware Guidance
- Start A Planning Conversation
- Ask What Needs Confirmation

CRM status:

- Permit-sensitive
- High-risk review

**State 5: Ready To Act**

Customer thought:

"I want to talk to the right company."

Best CTA:

- Request A Scope Review
- Start Your Project Conversation
- Call CMB

CRM status:

- Sales-qualified inquiry
- Response priority

**State 6: Repeat Customer**

Customer thought:

"They helped before. I want to use them again."

Best CTA:

- Start Another Project
- Upload Your Repair List
- Contact Your CMB Team

CRM status:

- Existing customer
- Relationship lead

### 3.3 Conversion Must Match State

Do not ask an exploring user for the same commitment as a ready-to-act user.

A modern product-style UX gives the user the right next step for their state. That is the Fantasy-style principle applied practically: modular pathways, not one blunt funnel.

## 4. CTA Architecture

### 4.1 CTA Hierarchy

Cochrane Master Builders should use a controlled CTA system.

**Primary CTA**

Used when the user is ready to start a real project conversation.

Recommended:

- Request A Scope Review
- Start Your Project Conversation
- Tell Us What You're Planning

**Secondary CTA**

Used when the user needs more clarity first.

Recommended:

- See What Affects Cost
- View The Process
- Read The Family Standard
- Explore Related Services

**Tertiary CTA**

Used for early-stage or educational users.

Recommended:

- Download A Checklist
- Read The Planning Guide
- Browse Services
- Compare Quote Scope

**Urgent CTA**

Used only when operations can handle urgency.

Recommended if true:

- Call About An Urgent Repair
- Request Repair Triage

Do not use emergency language unless the company can support it.

### 4.2 CTA Rules

- Every CTA must tell the truth about what happens next.
- Do not use "free quote" as the default for complex work.
- Do not use pressure language unless there is a real operational reason.
- Do not make every button the same.
- Match CTA to page intent.
- Match CTA to CRM workflow.
- Track every CTA.

### 4.3 Page-Level CTA Mapping

| Page Type | Primary CTA | Secondary CTA |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Find Your Project | See The Family Standard |
| Service page | Request A Scope Review | See What Affects Cost |
| Pricing guide | Request A Scope Review | Compare Quote Scope |
| Permit guide | Ask What Needs Confirmation | View Related Service |
| Project story | Plan A Similar Project | View Service Page |
| Family standard | Start With A Clear Scope | See The Process |
| Commercial page | Plan A Commercial Project | View Commercial Process |
| Repairs page | Upload Your Repair List | See Repair Guidance |
| Custom home page | Start A Planning Conversation | View Build Process |

### 4.4 CTA Microcopy

Use short expectation-setting copy under important CTAs.

Examples:

- "We will help identify the right next step based on project type, location, scope, and what needs to be confirmed."
- "Photos can help start the conversation, but complex work may still need a site review."
- "Pricing guidance is not a final quote. It helps you understand the variables before committing."
- "If this project may involve permits or licensed trades, we will flag what should be confirmed."

### 4.5 CTA Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- Get Started Now on every page;
- Claim Your Free Quote;
- Limited Time Offer;
- Instant Estimate;
- Guaranteed Price;
- We Do It All;
- Book Today Before It's Too Late;
- Talk To A Sales Rep.

These either cheapen the brand or create operational risk.

## 5. Form Architecture

### 5.1 Forms Are Trust Interfaces

A form is not a data collection box.

It is the first operational proof that the company is organized.

A good form makes the customer feel:

- heard;
- guided;
- not overburdened;
- safe sharing context;
- clear on what happens next.

A bad form makes the customer feel:

- like a lead;
- unsure what information matters;
- worried no one will respond;
- pressured to provide too much;
- confused by generic fields.

### 5.2 Two-Form Model

Use two core forms.

**Quick Inquiry Form**

For low-friction contact.

Fields:

- name;
- phone;
- email;
- project type;
- location/service area;
- short message;
- preferred contact method.

Best for:

- mobile users;
- urgent questions;
- early-stage inquiries;
- repair requests.

**Scope Review Form**

For qualified project planning.

Fields:

- name;
- phone;
- email;
- project address or area;
- customer type;
- project type;
- project goal;
- current condition;
- desired timeline;
- budget comfort range;
- photos/upload;
- permit concerns;
- biggest worry;
- decision-maker status;
- best contact method.

Best for:

- renovations;
- basements;
- suites;
- custom homes;
- commercial;
- complex repairs;
- high-value work.

### 5.3 Multi-Step Form Flow

For scope review, use steps:

1. Contact details.
2. Project type and location.
3. Project goal and current condition.
4. Timeline and budget orientation.
5. Photos and concerns.
6. Submit and expectation confirmation.

This feels easier than one long form.

### 5.4 Form Microcopy

**Budget Field**

"Budget ranges help us guide the conversation. They do not lock you into a number. Final pricing depends on scope, site conditions, access, materials, permits or licensed trades where applicable, and hidden conditions."

**Photo Upload**

"Photos help us understand the starting point. They do not replace a site review where hidden conditions, structure, access, or code-sensitive work may matter."

**Permit Concern**

"If you are unsure whether permits apply, tell us. We can help identify questions that should be confirmed with the proper authority."

**After Submission**

"Thank you. We received your project details. The next step is to review fit, service area, scope, and what may need confirmation before pricing or scheduling."

### 5.5 Form Routing Tags

Every form should create CRM tags:

- service cluster;
- service type;
- customer type;
- location;
- urgency;
- budget band;
- project stage;
- permit-sensitive;
- photos uploaded;
- commercial/residential;
- repeat/new customer;
- lead source;
- page source.

## 6. Lead Qualification System

### 6.1 Qualification Purpose

Qualification is not about rejecting people rudely.

It is about protecting:

- the customer;
- the schedule;
- the team;
- the family name;
- the estimate process;
- trade partners;
- future proof quality.

### 6.2 Lead Qualification Stages

**Stage 1: Raw Inquiry**

A new form, call, email, message, or referral.

Required action:

- capture source;
- confirm service type;
- check location;
- check urgency;
- assign owner.

**Stage 2: Qualified Inquiry**

Enough detail exists to determine whether the project may fit.

Required action:

- assess service fit;
- assess project stage;
- identify missing information;
- send next-step message.

**Stage 3: Scope Review**

The project needs structured review.

Required action:

- collect details;
- request photos;
- identify site visit need;
- flag permit/trade issues;
- determine estimate path.

**Stage 4: Sales-Qualified Opportunity**

The project is a fit and worth proposal effort.

Required action:

- schedule call/site visit;
- assign estimator/project lead;
- document scope assumptions;
- prepare estimate/proposal.

**Stage 5: Proposal Sent**

Customer has received pricing/scope.

Required action:

- follow up;
- answer questions;
- clarify exclusions;
- document revisions;
- update stage.

**Stage 6: Won / Lost / Nurture**

Project outcome recorded.

Required action:

- update reason;
- store learning;
- create follow-up;
- request feedback where appropriate.

### 6.3 Fit Scoring

Score each qualified lead from 1–5 on:

- service fit;
- location fit;
- timeline fit;
- budget fit;
- decision-maker clarity;
- project clarity;
- permit/compliance risk;
- communication fit;
- long-term value;
- proof opportunity.

### 6.4 Red Flag Tags

Use tags such as:

- cheapest-only;
- permit-avoidance;
- unrealistic timeline;
- unclear decision-maker;
- no budget discussion;
- scope too vague;
- service not offered;
- outside area;
- high conflict risk;
- asks for unsupported claims.

### 6.5 High-Fit Tags

Use tags such as:

- values clarity;
- family home project;
- repeat potential;
- permits respected;
- budget realistic;
- photos provided;
- decision-maker clear;
- local referral;
- proof opportunity;
- long-term property owner.

### 6.6 Qualification Script

"Thanks for sharing this. Before we talk numbers, we want to make sure we understand the scope properly. A responsible next step depends on project type, site conditions, materials, timeline, budget range, and whether permits or qualified trades may be involved. We will help identify whether this can move to estimate, needs a site review, or should start with planning guidance."

## 8. Lead Routing System

### 8.1 Routing Purpose

Routing prevents good leads from getting lost and bad-fit leads from consuming too much attention.

### 8.2 Routing By Service Cluster

Assign inquiry owner or path by cluster:

- custom homes / major builds;
- renovations;
- basements / suites;
- exterior envelope;
- outdoor living;
- concrete / excavation;
- interiors / finishes;
- repairs / maintenance;
- commercial;
- accessibility;
- real estate / pre-sale;
- seasonal.

### 8.3 Routing By Risk

High-risk leads require senior review.

High-risk flags:

- permit-sensitive;
- licensed-trade dependency;
- structural change;
- commercial code complexity;
- urgent safety concern;
- large budget;
- dispute or prior contractor issue;
- insurance-related;
- tight timeline;
- unclear ownership.

### 8.4 Routing By Customer Type

Different customer types need different handling.

**Homeowner**

Needs clarity, trust, disruption planning.

**Acreage Owner**

Needs access, site condition, weather, utility, and distance considerations.

**Investor / Landlord**

Needs ROI, durability, documentation, speed, compliance awareness.

**Local Business**

Needs schedule, downtime reduction, lease/landlord coordination, trade sequencing.

**Realtor / Pre-Sale**

Needs fast triage, inspection list clarity, photo documentation, practical scope.

**Repeat Customer**

Needs recognition, history, continuity, and faster context.

### 8.5 Routing Response Times

Define internal targets by urgency.

Example categories:

- urgent repair triage;
- standard inquiry;
- complex project;
- future planning;
- commercial inquiry;
- referral/repeat customer.

Do not publish response guarantees unless operations can consistently meet them.

### 8.6 Routing Failure Review

Review monthly:

- missed leads;
- late responses;
- wrong service routing;
- unqualified sales calls;
- poor-fit leads;
- inquiry source issues;
- form confusion;
- CRM gaps.

## 9. Follow-Up System

### 9.1 Follow-Up Is Reputation

Customers often judge a contractor before any work begins.

If follow-up is late, vague, or disorganized, they assume the project may feel the same.

### 9.2 Follow-Up Cadence

**New Inquiry**

- immediate confirmation;
- human review;
- first response target internally defined;
- next step assigned.

**Awaiting Info**

- follow up once with helpful context;
- follow up again with deadline/soft close;
- move to nurture if no response.

**Proposal Sent**

- confirm receipt;
- follow up with scope clarification;
- answer questions;
- record objections;
- set decision date;
- move to won/lost/nurture.

**Lost Lead**

- record reason;
- send respectful close message;
- nurture if appropriate;
- learn from price/scope/fit objections.

**Completed Project**

- closeout message;
- review request;
- maintenance/care note;
- future service path;
- seasonal reminder if relevant.

### 9.3 Follow-Up Message Templates

**New Inquiry Confirmation**

Thank you for reaching out to Cochrane Master Builders. We received your project details. The next step is to review the service type, location, scope, timeline, and what may need to be confirmed before pricing or scheduling. We will follow up with the responsible next step.

**Awaiting Photos**

Photos can help us understand the starting point. If you are able, please send wide shots of the area, close-ups of the issue, and any access or surrounding conditions. Photos do not replace a site review for complex work, but they help us guide the next step.

**Proposal Follow-Up**

We wanted to check that you received the scope and pricing information. Before comparing quotes, make sure you are comparing inclusions, exclusions, assumptions, materials, permits or licensed trades where applicable, and change-order process. Let us know what you would like clarified.

**Lost Lead Respectful Close**

Thank you for considering Cochrane Master Builders. If you move forward elsewhere, we still recommend keeping written scope, exclusions, permits or trade responsibilities where applicable, and change-order terms clear before work begins. If your needs change later, we are happy to reconnect.

### 9.4 Follow-Up Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- "Just checking in" with no value;
- repeated pressure;
- guilt language;
- disappearing after proposal;
- no lost reason tracking;
- private unlogged follow-up;
- no closeout message.

## 10. Sales Conversation System

### 10.1 Sales Is Guidance

For this brand, sales should not feel like persuasion.

It should feel like responsible guidance.

The salesperson or owner should help the customer understand:

- what they are trying to accomplish;
- what matters most;
- what scope is realistic;
- what affects pricing;
- what risks exist;
- what next step is responsible;
- whether CMB is the right fit.

### 10.2 Sales Call Structure

1. Confirm project and location.
2. Ask why now.
3. Ask what a good outcome looks like.
4. Ask what concerns them most.
5. Clarify scope.
6. Discuss timeline.
7. Discuss budget range respectfully.
8. Identify permit/trade/hidden-condition concerns.
9. Explain next step.
10. Confirm follow-up.

### 10.3 Key Questions

- What are you hoping this project changes for your home or business?
- What is not working right now?
- What worries you most about hiring a contractor?
- Have you had quotes or work done before?
- Is there a deadline driving this?
- Are there any inspection, permit, insurance, landlord, or resale concerns?
- What budget range are you trying to stay within?
- Who else is involved in the decision?
- What would make this feel successful six months after completion?

### 10.4 Budget Conversation

Use:

"Budget helps us guide the conversation. It does not mean we will spend every dollar. It helps us understand whether the desired scope, finish level, and timing are realistic, or whether we should discuss phases or trade-offs."

Do not use:

"What's your budget?" without context.

### 10.5 Fit Disqualification

Respectful disqualification examples:

- "Based on what you shared, this may not be the right fit for our process."
- "We would not want to promise a timeline we cannot responsibly support."
- "If the priority is the lowest possible number regardless of scope or documentation, we may not be the best match."
- "This work may require a qualified specialist or permit review before a contractor should proceed."

## 11. Estimate and Proposal Governance

### 11.1 Proposal Purpose

A proposal is not just a price.

It is a written trust agreement.

It should show:

- scope;
- inclusions;
- exclusions;
- assumptions;
- materials;
- allowances;
- timeline assumptions;
- permit/trade responsibilities;
- change-order process;
- payment terms;
- next step.

### 11.2 Proposal Sections

Recommended proposal structure:

1. Project summary.
2. Customer goal.
3. Scope included.
4. Scope excluded.
5. Materials / allowances.
6. Site conditions observed.
7. Unknowns / assumptions.
8. Permit or licensed-trade notes.
9. Schedule assumptions.
10. Price and payment structure.
11. Change-order process.
12. Customer responsibilities.
13. Acceptance terms.
14. Family standard note.

### 11.3 Proposal Language

Use:

- "included";
- "excluded";
- "assumed";
- "to be confirmed";
- "allowance";
- "scope change";
- "requires approval."

Avoid:

- vague package language;
- hidden exclusions;
- "miscellaneous" without explanation;
- verbal-only promises;
- overly legal coldness without human explanation.

### 11.4 Proposal Review Before Sending

Check:

- correct customer name;
- correct address;
- correct scope;
- exclusions clear;
- assumptions clear;
- pricing consistent;
- compliance language safe;
- timeline not overpromised;
- family standard included;
- next step clear.

### 11.5 Lost Proposal Review

Track why proposals are lost:

- price;
- timeline;
- scope mismatch;
- competitor chosen;
- no decision;
- budget mismatch;
- communication issue;
- unclear value;
- project postponed;
- outside service fit.

Use this data to improve content, pricing guidance, service pages, and qualification.

- commercial project handoff;

- customer is upset;

"Thank you for trusting Cochrane Master Builders with your project. If the process felt clear and the work met the standard you expected, a short Google review helps other Cochrane homeowners understand what it is like to work with us. ## 19. Reputation Risk and Recovery

### 19.1 Reputation Recovery Philosophy

Mistakes will happen.

The family standard is proven by how the company responds.

A strong recovery system can preserve trust even when something goes wrong.

### 19.2 Early Warning Signals

Track:

- delayed responses;
- repeated customer confusion;
- unresolved concerns;
- scope disputes;
- trade partner complaints;
- messy site feedback;
- late change orders;
- payment tension;
- negative review threats;
- staff avoidance of difficult calls.

### 19.3 Recovery Steps

1. Acknowledge concern.
2. Clarify facts.
3. Review documentation.
4. Identify responsibility.
5. Present next action.
6. Set timeline.
7. Follow up.
8. Document resolution.
9. Review system failure.
10. Add lesson to governance review.

### 19.4 Public Response Standard

If public review issue arises:

- respond calmly;
- do not reveal private details;
- acknowledge concern;
- invite direct discussion;
- avoid arguing;
- avoid blaming;
- reinforce commitment to review.

### 19.5 Recovery Template

"We are sorry to hear this concern. We take project communication and workmanship seriously. We will review the project details and would like to connect directly so we can understand what happened and discuss the appropriate next step."

18. Do not skip closeout documentation.
